00Overview

This is the polished, reusable artefact of a session delivered at Architect Dreamin' Helsinki 2026. Everything you need to re-run it with your own teams is on this page or in the repository.

If you've ever worked on a Salesforce transformation, chances are you've seen a roadmap once – at the kick-off – and then watched it quietly vanish like a post-go-live celebration cake. Seasoned architects know better. A well-crafted, high-quality roadmap isn't just a pretty slide; it is a power tool for aligning business ambition with technical delivery.

The session unpacks four roadmap types, drops teams into a deliberately demanding case study (Victory Fashion), then moves the goalposts on them. The point is not to produce a finished roadmap. The point is to learn how a good one absorbs change.

A roadmap that cannot change is a poster. A roadmap that changes transparently is a power tool.

What's in the kit

  • Theory – what a roadmap is and is not, the four types, the traceability rule, framed via Salesforce Well-Architected.
  • Scenario – Victory Fashion, a fictional global retailer in the middle of a TikTok-driven digital transformation.
  • Distractor cards – two general distractors plus ten per-team curveballs, ready to print and cut.
  • Facilitator playbook – full beat-by-beat run-of-show for 90 minutes, with a 2-hour variant.
  • Four worked SVG examples – including the cautionary roadmap_final_v7_FINAL(2).pptx anti-pattern.

01Why Roadmapping

Three symptoms appear in every failed programme we have seen. Roadmaps are the cheapest insurance against them.

The Standish Group's CHAOS Report puts roughly 24% of all IT projects in the "failed" bucket, with a further ~50% "challenged". The trend has not improved meaningfully across the Waterfall, Agile, Scrum, SAFe waves of the last two decades.

Unverified

Confirm the exact current figure against the latest Standish CHAOS Report before quoting it on stage.

Roadmaps don't fix delivery on their own. But every failed programme we have personally seen had the same three symptoms:

  • Scope creep. The board kept adding asks; nobody could show what would drop out.
  • Mismanaged dependencies. Teams discovered them three weeks before a milestone.
  • Stakeholders who had forgotten why. Six months in, the answer to "why are we building this again?" was no longer obvious.

A good roadmap addresses all three. It makes the "why" visible to executives, the sequencing visible to delivery, and the dependencies visible to everybody. It is not a project plan; it is a decision-support artefact.

What a roadmap is, and is not

Roadmaps are…

  • Plans from the "as-is" to the "to-be" state
  • Capabilities assigned to incremental delivery
  • Visibility into a foreseeable future
  • Oriented toward business value
  • Focused on target audience and purpose
  • A single view

Roadmaps are NOT…

  • Statements of Work or requirements documents
  • A backlog of user stories or epics
  • Long-term, rigid plans
  • Task-oriented like a project plan
  • Too detail-focused
  • The answer to everything

02Roadmap Types

Four types: three useful tools, one cautionary example. Match the type to the audience and the question.

1 · Business Capability Roadmap

Audience: executives, sponsors, business leadership.
Question it answers: "What must the organisation be able to do, and by when?"

Solution-agnostic. Swimlanes by business area (Sales, Marketing, Service, Platform). Capabilities like Guided Selling, Journey Orchestration, Self-Service Portal, Single Customer View. Dependencies shown as dashed arcs. This is the artefact that wins and retains executive buy-in. Keep it on one slide; do not put technology names on it.

2 · Business Feature Roadmap

Audience: product owners, delivery leads, business analysts.
Question it answers: "Within each capability, what features ship in which quarter, and what is at risk?"

Gantt-style feature bars across quarters. Status colour-coded: blue (on track) and amber (at risk). Dependency arrows make sequencing explicit. Example chain: Identity & SSO → Case deflection bot → Entitlement checks.

3 · Technology System Roadmap

Audience: architects, delivery teams, infrastructure and security leads.
Question it answers: "What must the platform deliver, in what order, to make the business roadmap possible?"

Swimlanes by technical concern: Operations, Infrastructure, Compliance, Security. Sequencing of migrations, audits, integrations, and enablers. Long-lived architecture decisions live here. The dark blocks at Q4 are the milestones the business roadmap depends on.

4 · The anti-pattern: roadmap_final_v7_FINAL(2).pptx

Audience: nobody, although it gets shown to everybody.
Question it answers: none.

Overlapping bars with labels like CRM thing phase 1?, Big Integration, Data clean-up again, AI something. Sticky notes saying ASAP!!, TBD, ?. Quarters out of order: Q1, Q3?, Q2, ??. Every team in the room recognises a version of this from their own working life.

The traceability rule

Frame the Business Capability roadmap and the Technology System roadmap along the Salesforce Well-Architected framework. The Business Capability roadmap keeps the "why" visible; the Technology System roadmap carries sequencing, dependencies, and long-lived architecture decisions. Every line on the technology roadmap must trace back to at least one business capability. No orphans.

03The Scenario · Victory Fashion

A fictional global retailer in the middle of a TikTok-driven digital transformation. Built for this workshop. Print one copy per participant.

Note

Victory Fashion is a fictional scenario. Any resemblance to real fashion retailers, K-Pop bands, or doomed legacy commerce platforms is entirely intentional.

The company

Victory is a global fashion retailer supplying clothing to over 11,000 department stores and 500 Victory retail stores through a full-package production model. Manufacturing is outsourced to partners in Asia and Turkey. Their success has depended on planning seasonal orders 9–12 months in advance, managed through Salesforce Classic.

What just happened

A recent viral TikTok moment featuring a K-Pop band has driven a surge in Gen Z demand for Victory's products. The surge has exposed serious limitations in their outdated systems and long lead times. To seize the moment, Victory has appointed a new CDO and CTO to drive digital transformation.

Goals:

  1. Launch a modern direct-to-consumer online store.
  2. Modernise operations end-to-end.
  3. Accelerate the product cycle – halve the time from production to sale.

Analysts forecast elevated demand for up to two years. The technology strategy has been approved by leadership and board. The CTO is now responsible for the technology roadmap. That is what your team will draft today.

Current systems

SystemPurposeState
Salesforce CRMMainly B2B connections to the 11,000 department storesClassic, in active use
Multiple regional ERPsOrder, inventory, finance per regionDoing their job, not in scope for replacement
Old Fashioned Commerce ShopDirect sales siteEarly 2000s, never touched, no modern payment providers
Custom PortalSupplier design-draft uploads and complaint managementBespoke, brittle, costly to extend
Design Tool3rd-party web app for designers, Victory-brandedCentrally managed, working well

The as-is landscape

Salesforce sits at the centre-right as the B2B management system, connected via an integration layer to the regional ERPs. The Commerce Shop, the Custom Portal and the Design Tool sit visibly disconnected on the left. The visual disconnection is the point of the diagram.

Capabilities to consider

The raw material for the teams' roadmaps. The job is to select, sequence, group, and prioritise across quarters, with explicit dependencies.

Sourcing & Designing

  • Integrated PLM with version control
  • Real-time collaboration across global design teams
  • AI-assisted design tool integration (seasonal colours, materials)
  • Supplier-portal integration for rapid prototype feedback

Supply chain & supplier management

  • Unified SRM
  • Self-service supplier onboarding and compliance validation
  • Automated milestone tracking (sample approvals, shipping status)
  • Embedded analytics for vendor performance and risk

Inventory tracking

  • Near real-time inventory visibility via ERP integrations
  • Predictive inventory allocation from sales/demand signals
  • Alert-based replenishment
  • Cross-channel stock sync (B2B and future B2C)

Sales & Customer Experience

  • Modern headless commerce platform, mobile-first UX
  • Modern payment gateways and fraud detection
  • Personalisation engine
  • Social commerce and influencer-driven flash sales

Contracting

  • Centralised CLM
  • Pre-approved legal templates and digital signature
  • Automated renewal and compliance tracking
  • Contract-to-invoice integration

Commercial & Marketing

  • Unified CDP for B2B and future B2C segmentation
  • AI-driven personal-shopper agent generating store "look-books"
  • Floor-plan generation and placement scoring
  • Performance marketing analytics dashboards

Operations (automation & AI)

  • Workflow automation (design approvals, supplier updates)
  • AI for seasonal demand forecasting and production planning
  • RPA for order reconciliation
  • Exception management alerts in logistics and fulfilment

Governance (fed by Insights & Intelligence)

  • Enterprise data lake / warehouse with BI layer
  • Role-based dashboards (merchandising, sourcing, finance)
  • Audit trails and data-governance policy enforcement

The team's task

In roughly 45 minutes: pick a roadmap type (capability, feature, or system), choose a horizon, place capabilities on swimlanes with at least one explicit dependency, and be ready to say – for every item on the board – which business goal it serves. No orphans.

04Distractor Cards

Two general distractors plus ten per-team curveballs. The point is to make teams visibly adapt their roadmap – not restart it.

Distribution

Every team receives both general distractors in Round 1, plus one unique per-team distractor. Ten minutes before the end of build time, drop the same final distractor on every team.

General · everybody gets these

G1 · General

International tariffs

The government of Victory's largest export market has announced new import tariffs of 18% on finished apparel, effective the start of next quarter. Half the supply contracts assume the old rates. Procurement want to re-route through Turkey; Finance wants to re-price. The board meets on Friday.

At your table:
  • What moves on your roadmap, and what gets cut?
  • Which technical capability suddenly becomes urgent?
  • Which stakeholder do you call first?

G2 · General

Data breach from the customer portal

The supplier-facing Custom Portal has been breached overnight. Design drafts for two upcoming seasons may be in the wild. PR will field questions by lunchtime. Legal is on the line about GDPR notification windows. The CISO wants the portal off the public internet by end of day.

At your table:
  • Does anything on your roadmap accelerate?
  • Does anything get paused or replaced?
  • Where does Trusted show up on your board now?

Per team · one each

T1

New technology in the market

A French startup, Heuritech, has released a fashion-trend prediction engine with claimed 70% accuracy on six-month seasonal trend calls. Two competitors have signed up in the last fortnight. The CDO is "very keen to understand our position".

At your table:
  • Does this go on the roadmap, and at what cost?
  • How do you defend "not yet" if that is the right answer?
  • Which existing capability would make this easy to add later?

T2

New wholesale channel

A regional buying group representing 1,800 independent boutiques has approached Victory with a wholesale partnership. They want EDI integration, real-time stock, and B2B portal access by the start of next quarter.

At your table:
  • Does your planned B2C work serve this, or are you running two programmes?
  • What gets re-sequenced to free up integration capacity?
  • What do you tell the CFO?

T3

New CEO with B2C focus

The board has appointed a new CEO with a strong direct-to-consumer pedigree from a digitally native brand. The CTO has been asked to "walk her through the roadmap on Wednesday and explain how we are accelerating the consumer story". The B2B sponsors are quietly nervous.

At your table:
  • Which items suddenly look under-invested? Over-invested?
  • How do you tell the B2B story without sounding defensive?
  • Does the audience for your roadmap change?

T4

Regulatory change: EU AI Act

Several of your AI ambitions – the personal-shopper agent, AI-assisted design, seasonal demand forecasting – are likely to be classified as "limited" or "high" risk under the EU AI Act, with documentation, transparency, and human-oversight obligations. Compliance want a written plan within a month.

At your table:
  • Which AI items stay, which pause, which need a compliance workstream?
  • Does a new swimlane appear on your roadmap?
  • How do you make Trusted visible to the board?

T5

ERP upgrade (EMEA only)

The EMEA regional ERP team have announced an upgrade to the next major version, mandatory within the next two quarters. Expect a freeze on new integrations during the window, and a behaviour change in the inventory API afterwards. Other regions are not affected.

At your table:
  • Which inventory items hit the freeze window?
  • Accelerate, delay, or split by region?
  • What does this do to the "single view" promise?

T6

New luxury line of business

Leadership have approved the launch of a new premium sub-brand, Victory Atelier – appointment-only, made-to-measure, hand-finished. First capsule drops in nine months. The CDO wants to know whether the existing roadmap "covers it".

At your table:
  • Does Atelier share capabilities with core Victory, or need its own swimlane?
  • Which existing items are genuinely reusable?
  • How do you communicate the answer?

T7

Key supplier breaches anti-slavery law

Investigative journalists have tied one of Victory's three largest manufacturing partners to forced labour. The board's position is immediate: the relationship ends today. Procurement need to redistribute the volume; PR are preparing a statement; the new ESG officer wants every supplier on the books re-audited.

At your table:
  • Which capability is suddenly the most important one?
  • What does this do to the SRM items?
  • How do you embed governance without making it a separate ignored lane?

T8

Cash-flow constraint

An unexpectedly bad season in outerwear has put pressure on cash. The CFO has cut capex for the next two quarters by 30%, with the option to protect "one revenue-generating capability" on the roadmap.

At your table:
  • Which one do you protect, and how do you defend it?
  • What gets paused, and what gets killed outright?
  • How do you preserve sequencing so cuts can come back?

T9

File storage reaches capacity

The platform team have raised a P1: shared file storage holding design drafts, supplier documents, and historical commerce assets is at 94% and growing. Procurement of additional capacity takes a quarter. Migration to cloud object storage has been "planned" for two years.

At your table:
  • Does this technical-debt item make it onto the board?
  • Which business capability does it actually unlock?
  • How do you talk about tech debt to a board that does not want to hear about it?

T10

Design tool vendor acquired

The 3rd-party design tool you depend on has been acquired by a large platform vendor, which will expose design assets externally to "influencers and creators" via a marketplace in six months. Designers are nervous. Marketing is intrigued. Legal is reading the new terms now.

At your table:
  • Risk item, opportunity item, or both?
  • What does it do to the AI-assisted design tool capability?
  • Build a replacement now, or exploit the change?

Final distractor · everybody gets the same

The default is T3 · New CEO with B2C focus, dropped on every team 10 minutes before the end of build time. It is the highest-impact card because it forces teams to ask whether the audience for the roadmap has changed – the real test of whether they understood the theory in Block 1.

Choose a different card if your room is leaning a particular way: T4 for an AI-heavy audience, T7 for a sustainability-focused one, T8 for an audience that has been ignoring constraints.

05Facilitator Playbook

Beat-by-beat run-of-show for 90 minutes. Read end-to-end before you facilitate. Full markdown source in materials/facilitator-playbook.md.

Materials & room setup

  • One copy of the Victory scenario per participant. Print double-sided.
  • One full set of distractor cards per team. Cut, sort into three piles: General, Per-team, Final.
  • Flip-chart paper, coloured markers, sticky notes per team.
  • The four SVG examples on screen, anti-pattern queued up last.
  • Tables of 3–6 people, mixed roles. Six is the upper limit.

Block 1 · Intro & theory (0:00–0:15)

Beat 1.1 · The hook

You say"Show of hands – who in this room has been on a programme where you saw the roadmap once, at the kick-off, and then watched it quietly vanish?"

Beat 1.2 · The numbers

Show the Standish CHAOS trend. Headline: ~24% of all IT projects considered failed, ~50% challenged, no meaningful improvement across the methodology waves.

Beat 1.3 · What a roadmap is and is not

Two columns on the slide. Read them straight. Resist softening the "is NOT" column – that is where recognition lives.

Beat 1.4 · The four types

Show the SVGs in order: capability, feature, system, anti-pattern. Drop this line when the anti-pattern lands:

You say"Every team in this room has shipped a version of this. The point of today is to send you home with the tools to never ship one again."

Beat 1.5 · The traceability rule

Frame Business Capability vs Technology System via Well-Architected pillars. Every line on the tech roadmap traces back to at least one business capability. No orphans.

Block 2 · Scenario build (0:15–0:45)

Beat 2.1 · Form teams and hand out

You say"Read together for five minutes. Before you draw anything, agree at the table: which type of roadmap, and why for this audience. Write your answer in the corner of your sheet."

Beat 2.2 · The build (25 min)

Circulate. Carry a marker but do not draw. Ask one of: "Who is the audience?" "Which goal does this line serve?" "What is your single explicit dependency?" Do not correct over-engineering – let the distractors do that work in Block 3.

Block 3 · Distractors round 1 (0:45–1:00)

Beat 3.1 · The drop

Move to each table with G1, G2, and one Per-team card. Read the narrative out loud at the table – do not just hand the cards over. The voicing matters.

Beat 3.2 · Adapt, do not restart

The most important coaching moment in the whole session: stop teams that try to redraw from scratch. "Put the old items in red; draw the new ones in green over the top."

Block 4 · Final distractor (1:00–1:10)

Beat 4.1 · The curveball

You say"The board has just appointed a new CEO with a strong direct-to-consumer pedigree. Wednesday she wants to be walked through your roadmap and told how it accelerates the consumer story. You have eight minutes."

Beat 4.2 · The squeeze

Capability-first roadmaps adapt by re-weighting. Thinly disguised project plans start scrabbling. Do not rescue. The difference is the lesson.

Block 5 · Team presentations (1:10–1:25)

Beat 5.1 · The frame

You say"Two minutes per team. Which roadmap type, who is the audience, and – the important one – how did it change as the world moved on you? Save the original for last."

Rubric

CriterionQuestionWatch for
TraceabilityDoes every line connect to a business capability?Orphans; tech-debt disguised as features
SequencingAre dependencies explicit, and do they survive distractors?Bars on top of each other with no order
Audience fitDoes the roadmap match its stated audience?Exec roadmaps with technical jargon
AdaptabilityHow gracefully were distractors absorbed?Whole-sheet redraws (bad); coloured-over deltas (good)

Block 6 · Close & harvest (1:25–1:30)

A roadmap that cannot change is a poster. A roadmap that changes transparently is a power tool. Everything we did today was to give you the second one.

Three popcorn prompts: "One thing you'll do differently." "One thing this session got wrong about your reality." "One distractor we should add for next time."

Flex & contingency

  • Short on time: skip Beats 1.1 and 1.2; drop Block 4; run Block 5 as a share-out.
  • Extra time: add a 15-minute mid-build "stakeholder interview" – facilitator plays the new CEO; teams pitch as-is for 2 minutes.
  • Stuck team: sketch the four-swimlane skeleton on a fresh sheet, silently. They almost always restart from there.
  • Over-confident team: drop a second per-team distractor quietly. T7 or T8 usually do it.
